Insted of fighting man to man with swords or arrows, cannons (first made in the 16th Century) could now be made to shoot objects from a longer distance and do more damage.

The Wireless Radio Station

The development of the radio in the late 1800's greatly affected the ways orders were issued to soldiers in World War I and II. Colonels and Generals no longer had to be nearby to communicate quickly with their battalions.


Before radios were used, Colonels used dogs and pigeons to communicate with their troops. This was a risk because the animals may get shot or taken by the enemies to receive messages.


Unfortunately, radio was still very new in WWI, and many commanders liked to rely more on their old methods, which still worked, but were slower. News Stations, however, were able to use higher powered radios to receive information which could be reported from WWI. As time progressed, radio was becoming increasingly more relevant. By the end of the war, the United States Navy sent it's last dispatch of World War One, to announce armistice on November 11, 1918.

The Introduction of Poisonous Gas to WWI

The chlorine gas attacks at the Second Battle of Ypres in Belgium mark the beginning of Chemical Warfare. Five thousand troops were killed and at least ten thousand injured. Instead of attempting a "shoot to kill" method, the Germans' goals changed. They discovered that torture and death could both be done at the same time on a battlefield.


Chlorine and mustard gases quickly became feared elements in World War I because they were heavier than the air and a strong irritant to the skin, eyes, and especially the lungs. Extended exposure to the gases could lead to chemical burns, blindness, and/or long-term respiratory illnesses.


Poisonous gases quickly changed war strategies, and have since been banned (in 1972) from use in wars for their torturous consequences to health.

The Internal Combustion Engine

The improved Combustion Engine in the early 1900's was a breakthrough in transportation technology. When it was mass produced for tanks and warplanes in World War I, the advancement made an enormous difference in War Strategy. Planes could now air-lift doughboys and their allies over enemy lines and lead an attack from the other side! War planes were also fitted with machine guns to lead dog fights in the air or shoot down at the enemies.


Tanks could also now travel further distances on rougher terrain than their predecessor, the armored car. With more powerful engines, the tank had the power to help break the stalemate on the Western Front, in favor of the Allied Powers.
